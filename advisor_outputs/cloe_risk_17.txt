## 2) Risk vs diversification: how to delineate them

Use this rule:

- **Risk** asks: **“Where can losses come from?”**
- **Diversification** asks: **“How balanced is the allocation across independent drivers?”**

### Risk section
Should focus on:
- loss channels
- concentration that worsens downside
- protection / lack of protection
- maturity sensitivity
- FX sensitivity
- listed-market mark-to-market / liquidity channels
- opaque areas where losses are hard to localize

Examples from your sample:
- **17% in 5–10y fixed-income buckets** → rate / mark-to-market risk
- **18% FGC-eligible bank credit** vs non-FGC credit elsewhere
- **19.2% international economic exposure** → likely BRL FX sensitivity unless hedged
- **4% crypto** → distinct tail-risk bucket
- **22% mapped sovereign issuer concentration** → large single issuer, though of a different quality than corporate concentration

### Diversification section
Should focus on:
- spread across exposure groups
- issuer breadth
- country breadth
- wrapper breadth
- maturity dispersion
- dependence on one regime

Examples:
- CR1 = **30%**
- CR2 = **48.8%**
- issuer coverage only **45%**
- maturity coverage only **45%**
- effective exposure-group count ≈ **5.5**

### Important design rule
Some metrics are **shared primitives** but used differently.

Example:
- **Exposure-group concentration ratio** is primarily a **diversification** metric.
- It becomes a **risk** metric only after you interpret what that concentrated bucket can lose under stress.

So I’d keep:
- `risk_section`
- `diversification_section`

as **siblings**, not merged.

## 4) Brazilian context: what factors belong

### Yes: Selic risk
But name it carefully.

I’d use:
- `cdi_selic_regime_exposure`
- not just `selic risk`

Why:
- CDI / Selic-linked assets are not mainly “price crash” assets
- the relevant risk is more:
  - carry/reinvestment regime risk
  - opportunity-cost risk versus falling/rising rates
  - some mark-to-market if sold early in market-priced wrappers

Tesouro Direto states that early resale is at market value, prices reflect secondary-market conditions and interest-rate expectations, and higher market rates reduce bond prices. Tesouro Selic is also described by Tesouro Direto as having relatively low volatility and being suitable for emergency reserves. ([tesourodireto.com.br](https://www.tesourodireto.com.br/es/sobre-o-tesouro/regras-e-regulamento))

### Yes: inflation risk
Use:
- `inflation_linked_br`
- plus a separate modifier: `real_rate_duration`

Why:
- IPCA-linked bonds hedge inflation if held to maturity
- but they still have mark-to-market sensitivity to real-yield moves before maturity

So for risk, don’t stop at “IPCA exposure”; also ask:
- how much is in longer maturities?
- how much is subject to early-sale mark-to-market?

### Yes: credit risk, but split it properly
Don’t model “FGC vs non-FGC” as the risk factor itself.

Better split into:

#### Credit factor
- `sovereign_credit_br`
- `bank_credit_br`
- `corporate_credit_br`
- `international_credit`

#### Protection modifier
- `fgc_eligible`
- `fgc_limit_verified`
- `unprotected`
- `unknown_protection`

That is cleaner, because FGC is a **loss mitigant**, not a primary economic driver.

For this sample:
- **FGC-eligible bank credit** = **18%**
- **direct known non-FGC private credit** = at least **5%** (`debenture_infra`)
- plus whatever private credit is inside funds, if any, which is not observable here

### Yes: currency risk
Definitely.

But compute it from **economic exposure**, not just `country_bucket`.

Reason:
- `mixed_global_fund` is domiciled in Brazil, but its exposure is partly international
- so country bucket is about wrapper domicile, not true economic sensitivity

I’d use:
- `fx_sensitive_weight`
- `fx_hedged_weight`
- `fx_unhedged_weight`
- `fx_status_unknown_weight`

With current data, absent hedge instructions:
- international economic exposure = **19.2%**
- likely treat that as **FX-sensitive by default**, unless instruction text says hedged

### “B3 listing risk”: yes, but rename it
I would **not** use `b3_listing_risk` as a fundamental factor.

Better names:
- `listed_market_liquidity_br`
- `secondary_market_mark_to_market_br`
- `exchange_traded_wrapper_risk_br`

Reason:
- being listed on B3 is not an economic factor like inflation or FX
- it is a **transmission channel**:
  - daily price discovery
  - gap risk
  - liquidity risk
  - premium/discount behavior in wrappers
  - trading-halt / market-stress behavior

B3 itself frames stocks, ETFs and debentures through liquidity/risk criteria and revisable limits, which supports treating listing as a market/liquidity channel rather than a standalone macro factor. ([b3.com.br](https://www.b3.com.br/pt_br/produtos-e-servicos/compensacao-e-liquidacao/clearing/administracao-de-riscos/garantias/limites-de-renda-variavel-e-fixa/))

## My strongest design recommendations

1. **Call it a precursor, not a risk model.**
2. **Use raw ratios, not a black-box scalar score.**
3. **Separate factor, modifier, and protection.**
   - factor = inflation, Selic/CDI, equity, FX, crypto
   - modifier = maturity, issuer concentration, listing/liquidity
   - protection = FGC, hedge, guarantee text
4. **Track coverage gaps explicitly.**
   - unknown issuer weight
   - unknown maturity weight
   - unknown hedge status
5. **Keep diversification as a sibling section.**

Below is a design review focused on what a downstream AI would need to write a **defensible, non-speculative diversification section** for a **Brazilian investor**.

## Short verdict

Your current precursor is **good for broad economic-exposure commentary** and **not yet sufficient for robust concentration commentary**.

Right now, a downstream AI can safely say things like:

- the portfolio is split across CDI/IPCA/prefixado, Brazilian equities, international equities, Brazilian real estate, international bonds, and crypto;
- there is some international exposure;
- there is a mix of direct securities and pooled vehicles.

But it **cannot safely** say, without qualification:

- issuer concentration is good/bad;
- fixed-income maturities are well laddered;
- country diversification is strong;
- FGC protection is adequate;
- overlap inside ETFs/funds is low/high.

The main fixes are:
1. add **coverage-aware concentration metrics**,  
2. split **country of risk** from **vehicle domicile/listing**,  
3. add **applicability-aware coverage** (`unknown` vs `not_applicable`),  
4. add **machine-readable guardrails**.

---

# 1) What is missing?

## A. Applicability-aware coverage is missing
Your current `coverage_summary.json` is directionally useful, but it will mislead the downstream AI unless you distinguish:

- **covered**
- **unknown**
- **not applicable**

### Why this matters
Example: `maturity_coverage = 0.45`.

That looks weak if read against the whole portfolio, but maturity is really relevant mainly for **fixed income with defined maturity**. If the portfolio has **45% in direct fixed income**, then `0.45` might actually mean **100% of applicable fixed income is covered**.

Same issue for issuer:
- funds/ETFs often do **not** have a meaningful single economic issuer for diversification analysis;
- that is different from “issuer is missing due to bad mapping.”

### Recommendation
Replace each coverage metric with:

```json
{
  "coverage_on_total": 0.45,
  "applicable_weight": 0.50,
  "coverage_on_applicable": 0.90,
  "unknown_weight_within_applicable": 0.05,
  "not_applicable_weight": 0.50,
  "commentary_status": "partial"
}
```

Do this for at least:
- issuer
- issuer_type
- maturity
- FGC eligibility
- country of risk

---

## B. Concentration metrics are missing
The downstream AI should not have to infer concentration from raw tables.

## Must-have metrics
For each dimension, provide:

- **HHI**
- **effective number of bets** (`1 / HHI`)
- **top 1 / top 3 / top 5 concentration**
- **largest bucket**
- **unknown/not-applicable share**
- **coverage basis used**

### Dimensions
At minimum:

- positions
- economic exposure
- country of risk
- issuer type
- issuer
- fixed-income maturity buckets
- instrument kind / wrapper type

### Why
This lets the downstream AI say:
- “economic exposure is moderately diversified”
- “issuer concentration cannot be fully assessed”
- “international exposure exists but remains secondary”
without improvising.

### Concrete example from your sample
Using `allocation_by_economic_exposure.csv`, economic exposure HHI is about **0.181**, implying an effective diversification of roughly **5.5 buckets**.  
That is useful and safe.

But issuer HHI on total portfolio is **not useful unless coverage is handled properly**, because only **45%** is mapped to issuer.

---

## C. Unique counts are missing
Counts matter a lot in diversification commentary.

## Add:
- `position_count`
- `unique_economic_exposure_count`
- `unique_country_of_risk_count`
- `unique_issuer_count_known`
- `unique_issuer_type_count_known`
- `unique_issuer_count_on_applicable_universe`
- `count_direct_positions`
- `count_pooled_vehicles`

### Why
A portfolio with 14 positions can still be concentrated if exposures collapse into a few buckets.  
Counts plus HHI give a much better picture.

---

## D. Country needs to be split into multiple dimensions
Current `country_bucket` = `Brazil / Non-Brazil` is **too coarse** and also appears to mix **vehicle domicile** with **economic exposure**.

### Example from your sample
`mixed_global_fund` is marked as `country_bucket = Brazil`, but its economic exposures are:
- `eq_international: 0.6`
- `fi_cdi_br: 0.4`

So if the downstream AI uses current `country_bucket`, it will understate international exposure.

### In your sample
- By current row-level country bucket: **Brazil 81% / Non-Brazil 19%**
- By economic exposure look-through: about **Brazil 76.8% / Non-Brazil 23.2%**

That is a meaningful difference.

## Recommendation: separate these dimensions
Use **three distinct fields**:

1. **vehicle_domicile_country**
   - where the wrapper is domiciled / regulated
   - e.g. Brazil, Cayman, Luxembourg

2. **listing_venue**
   - e.g. B3, offshore, not listed

3. **country_of_risk** or **economic_geography**
   - where the underlying economic exposure lives

For country of risk, Brazil / Non-Brazil is still too coarse. Prefer buckets like:
- Brazil
- US
- Developed ex-US
- Emerging Markets
- Global / mixed
- Stateless / non-sovereign geographic exposure (useful for crypto)
- Unknown

For a Brazilian investor, this is much more useful than a binary split.

---

## E. The economic exposure code is too “flat”
`fi_cdi_br`, `fi_ipca_br`, `eq_international`, etc. are useful, but they compress multiple dimensions into one token.

## Better: decompose economic exposure into orthogonal attributes
For each exposure component, include:

```json
{
  "code": "fi_cdi_br",
  "weight": 1.0,
  "asset_class": "fixed_income",
  "subclass": "post_fixed",
  "indexer": "CDI",
  "inflation_linked": false,
  "geography": "Brazil",
  "currency_exposure": "BRL"
}
```

Do similarly for:
- IPCA-linked
- prefixado
- Brazilian equity
- international equity
- Brazilian real estate
- international bonds
- crypto

### Why
This allows the downstream AI to comment on:
- asset-class diversification
- rate-index diversification
- inflation-link diversification
- geographic diversification
without parsing code strings.

For Brazilian context, this is especially important because **CDI / IPCA / prefixado** are core diversification lenses.

---

## F. Fixed-income diversification fields are missing
For a Brazilian portfolio, diversification is not just “fixed income vs equity”.  
Within fixed income, the AI needs:

- **allocation by indexer**: CDI / IPCA / prefixado / foreign fixed income
- **allocation by maturity bucket**
- **allocation by issuer type** within fixed income
- **FGC-eligible vs non-FGC**
- **FGC conglomerate concentration**
- **covered vs uncovered fixed-income subset**

## Add these aggregates
- `allocation_by_fixed_income_indexer`
- `allocation_by_fixed_income_maturity_bucket`
- `allocation_by_fixed_income_issuer_type`
- `allocation_by_fgc_eligibility`
- `allocation_by_fgc_group`

### Important Brazilian nuance
FGC analysis is **amount-based**, not weight-only.  
If you want the downstream AI to discuss FGC concentration meaningfully, you also need:

- `portfolio_market_value_brl`
- `position_market_value_brl`
- `fgc_eligible_amount_brl`
- `fgc_group_code`

Without BRL amounts, the AI should **not** comment on whether FGC limits are sufficient.

---

## G. Allocation tables should include residual buckets
Today:

- `allocation_by_issuer_type.csv` sums to **50%**
- `allocation_by_issuer.csv` sums to **45%**

That is dangerous, because the downstream AI may read them as complete views.

## Recommendation
Every allocation table should sum to 100% of portfolio, using explicit buckets such as:

- `unknown`
- `not_applicable`
- `lookthrough_unavailable`

Example:

```csv
code,weight,pct,bucket_status
tesouro_nacional,0.22,22.00,known
banco_itau,0.10,10.00,known
banco_bradesco,0.08,8.00,known
petrobras,0.05,5.00,known
lookthrough_unavailable,0.50,50.00,not_applicable_or_unavailable
unknown_direct_issuer,0.05,5.00,unknown
```

This is one of the most important changes.

---

## H. Look-through level is missing
The downstream AI must know whether an exposure is:

- direct security
- wrapper-level only
- mandate-level estimate
- actual holdings look-through

## Add
Per position:

```json
{
  "lookthrough_level": "economic_exposure_only",
  "exposure_precision": "template_estimated"
}
```

### Why
Otherwise the AI may overstate precision on funds/ETFs.

---

## I. Human-readable labels are missing
The downstream AI only sees codes. That is brittle.

## Add:
- `label_pt_br`
- `short_definition_pt_br`

Examples:
- `fi_cdi_br` → “Renda fixa Brasil pós-fixada ao CDI”
- `fi_ipca_br` → “Renda fixa Brasil indexada ao IPCA”
- `eq_br` → “Renda variável Brasil”
- `re_br` → “Fundos/imóveis Brasil”
- `br_bank` → “Bancos brasileiros”
- `br_government` → “Governo brasileiro”

---

## J. Threshold flags should be precomputed, not improvised
If you want the downstream AI to state “concentrated” or “balanced”, do not force it to invent thresholds.

## Add policy-driven flags
Examples:
- single issuer above policy max
- single economic exposure above policy max
- crypto above policy max
- international exposure below target range
- fixed income overly concentrated in CDI
- maturity concentration in short bucket

These should be **config-driven**, not hard-coded as universal truths.

Example flag object:

```json
{
  "flag_code": "single_issuer_above_limit",
  "dimension": "issuer",
  "entity_code": "tesouro_nacional",
  "observed_weight": 0.22,
  "threshold": 0.20,
  "severity": "medium",
  "coverage_basis": "total_portfolio",
  "message_pt_br": "Exposição ao Tesouro Nacional acima do limite configurado."
}
```

# 2) Merge instruction text or keep separate blocks?

## Recommendation
**Keep them as separate labeled blocks with source and priority.**  
Optionally also generate a deterministic merged string for legacy prompt compatibility.

## Do not
Do **not** concatenate all five tables into one undifferentiated prose blob.

That loses:
- provenance,
- specificity,
- conflict visibility,
- ordering control.

## Best structure
Per position:

```json
"instructions_diversification": [
  {
    "source_table": "instrument_template",
    "source_code": "cdb_cdi_generic",
    "priority": 100,
    "label": "Template-specific guidance",
    "text": "..."
  },
  {
    "source_table": "economic_exposure",
    "source_code": "fi_cdi_br",
    "priority": 90,
    "label": "Exposure role",
    "text": "..."
  },
  {
    "source_table": "issuer",
    "source_code": "banco_itau",
    "priority": 80,
    "label": "Issuer-specific concentration note",
    "text": "..."
  },
  {
    "source_table": "issuer_type",
    "source_code": "br_bank",
    "priority": 70,
    "label": "Issuer-type note",
    "text": "..."
  },
  {
    "source_table": "tax_profile",
    "source_code": "general",
    "priority": 40,
    "label": "Tax nuance",
    "text": "..."
  }
]
```

## Best reading order
For downstream comprehension:

1. **global guardrails**
2. **coverage/limitations**
3. **portfolio-level concentration flags**
4. **position-level instructions**, ordered:
   - instrument_template
   - economic_exposure
   - issuer
   - issuer_type
   - tax_profile

### Why this order
- template is most concrete;
- exposure tells the portfolio role;
- issuer/issuer type informs concentration;
- tax is usually secondary for diversification.

## If you also want a merged narrative
Generate it deterministically with headings:

```text
[TEMPLATE]
...

[ECONOMIC EXPOSURE]
...

[ISSUER]
...

[ISSUER TYPE]
...

[TAX PROFILE]
...
```

# 3) Guardrails to prevent speculation

The precursor should include **machine-readable guardrails**, not just prose instructions.

## A. Topic permission flags
Add:

```json
"analysis_permissions": {
  "economic_exposure": "full",
  "country_of_risk": "full",
  "issuer": "partial",
  "issuer_type": "partial",
  "maturity": "partial",
  "lookthrough_holdings": "forbidden",
  "fgc_adequacy": "forbidden_without_amounts"
}
```

---

## B. Required qualifier rules
Example:

```json
"qualifier_rules": [
  {
    "dimension": "issuer",
    "if_coverage_below": 0.80,
    "required_phrase": "No nível de emissor, a análise cobre apenas a parcela mapeada da carteira."
  }
]
```

Better yet, store:
- required qualifier text in Portuguese,
- minimum coverage for unqualified commentary.

---

## C. Explicit prohibited inferences
Add a list like:

```json
"prohibited_inferences": [
  "Do not infer fund or ETF underlying holdings unless lookthrough_level = holdings.",
  "Do not infer sector concentration from ETF/fund names alone.",
  "Do not infer FGC sufficiency without BRL amounts and FGC group mapping.",
  "Do not treat vehicle domicile as country of economic risk.",
  "Do not make issuer-level claims on not_applicable or lookthrough_unavailable positions.",
  "Do not infer benchmark composition unless benchmark_label is explicitly provided."
]
```

---

## D. Unknown vs not applicable must be explicit
This is critical. The AI should know whether something is:
- truly missing,
- or not meaningful for that instrument.

Examples:
- maturity for equities: `not_applicable`
- issuer for look-through-unavailable ETF exposure: `lookthrough_unavailable`
- issuer missing on a debenture: `unknown`

These are not the same.

---

## E. Add denominator discipline
Every concentration claim should be tied to a denominator:
- total portfolio
- covered subset
- applicable universe

Example:
- good: “Tesouro Nacional representa 22% da carteira total e 48.9% da parcela com emissor identificado.”
- bad: “Tesouro é quase metade da carteira.”

## How to read the key sections

### `coverage`
Tells the downstream AI what it may discuss confidently.

### `analysis_permissions`
Simple machine-readable gatekeeping.

### `allocations`
Should always sum to 100% using explicit `known / unknown / not_applicable / lookthrough_unavailable` buckets where needed.

### `concentration_metrics`
Precomputed, so the AI does not invent math.

### `threshold_flags`
Lets the AI say “above configured limit” instead of making subjective judgments.

### `positions`
Preserves row-level evidence and source instructions.

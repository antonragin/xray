## 4.8 Investment funds — target state: one template per fund CNPJ

**Decision:**  
For investment funds, your **target state should be one template per actual fund / share class / CNPJ**.

I would **not** stop at generic strategy-only templates as the end state.

Why:
- 94 unique funds is actually manageable
- funds are exactly the kind of product where `cnpj`, `yearly_fee_pct`, `performance_fee_pct`, and tax behavior matter
- a generic template loses too much

So the practical answer is:

- **target state:** one template per fund CNPJ/name
- **migration fallback:** strategy-level generic templates for the long tail while you backfill

### Recommended code convention for dedicated funds
Best option:
- `fund_cnpj_<14digits>`

Alternative mnemonic option:
- `fund_<normalized_name>`

I strongly prefer **CNPJ-based** codes for funds, because names change more often than CNPJs.

### 1. Template Granularity: Generic vs. Specific
**Recommendation:** A hybrid approach. Use **Generic Templates** for direct credit/equities and **Specific Templates** for Investment Funds.

*   **Why Generic for FI/Credit/Equities?** The specification states: *"For fixed income... issuer are still supplied per uploaded row, not baked into the template."* You do not need 150 templates for CRAs. You need exactly 3 templates (`cra_pos_fixado`, `cra_ipca`, `cra_prefixado`), and you pass the issuer and maturity dynamically in the CSV. 
*   **Why Specific for Funds?** Funds have unique CNPJs, management/performance fees, and highly specific mixed economic exposures. A generic "multimarket fund" template fails to capture the true risk exposure of an individual fund.

### 2. Which Products Must Be Specific vs. Generic?
**MUST BE SPECIFIC (One template per product):**
*   **Investment Funds (94 templates):** Each of the 94 unique funds needs its own template. E.g., `fund_btg_cdb_plus_firf`, `fund_porto_di_firf`. This allows you to set `yearly_fee_pct`, map the exact `tax_profile` (FIA vs FIM), and use `economic_exposure_weights_json` if it's a mixed macro fund.
*   **Highly Complex ETFs/BDRs:** If an ETF has mixed exposure or specific fees, make it a template (e.g., `etf_ivvb11`, `etf_bova11`).

**MUST BE GENERIC (Shared templates):**

## 4. Paper FIIs
**Problem:** many people lazily map all FIIs to `re_br`.  
**Solution:** don’t. KNCR11/KNIP11/MCCI11-style vehicles should use FI exposure where appropriate.

## 5. Debênture incentivada vs regular
**Problem:** if upstream doesn’t distinguish them, tax analysis breaks.  
**Solution:** upstream must classify instrument tax status before producing `instrument_template_code`.

## 6. Tesouro Direto vs secondary-market NTN-B
**Problem:** teams may want separate templates because purchase channel differs.  
**Solution:** do not separate. Same economic product.

## 7. Listed-export category mixing
**Problem:** your “listed equities” bucket already includes ETFs and BDRs.  
**Solution:** split by actual wrapper before mapping:
- direct BR equity → `equity_br_listed_generic`
- BDR → `bdr_equity_listed_generic`
- ETF → dedicated ETF template

## 8. Fund naming instability
**Problem:** names change; codes are immutable.  
**Solution:** use `fund_cnpj_<digits>` whenever possible.

## 9. COEs with messy names
**Problem:** one generic `coe_unresolved` is too lossy.  
**Solution:** normalize to 19 product-type templates if possible; otherwise family-level fallback templates.

## 10. Issuer alias drift
**Problem:** no alias table exists in MVP.  
**Solution:** upstream parser must canonicalize issuer names before they hit X-Ray.

## 11. XP appears in two different roles
**Problem:** `XP` can mean a Brazilian bank/counterparty or the foreign listed company underlying `XPBR31`.  
**Solution:** use separate issuer rows:
- `banco_xp` → `br_bank`
- `xp_inc` → `nonbr_other`

## 12. Fund sponsor vs issuer confusion
**Problem:** BTG/Porto appearing in fund names may tempt operators to set fund issuer = bank.  
**Solution:** do **not** use sponsor/manager as `issuer` unless economically relevant. Product identity belongs in the template.
